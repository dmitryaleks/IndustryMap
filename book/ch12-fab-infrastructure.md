# Chapter 12: The Factory Behind the Factory

> A semiconductor fab is only as good as the infrastructure that moves wafers, holds them in place, feeds them chemicals, and tests the results -- and nearly all of that invisible infrastructure is Japanese.

## Introduction

When industry analysts quote the cost of a new leading-edge semiconductor fabrication plant -- typically between $15 billion and $25 billion -- the headline numbers conjure images of extreme ultraviolet lithography scanners, fleets of deposition chambers, and acres of cleanroom space. But behind every one of those marquee tools stands a vast, largely invisible ecosystem of companies that make the fab actually function. Someone must build the automated highways that move wafers between 600 to 1,000 process steps. Someone must manufacture the robotic arms that load and unload those wafers with sub-millimeter precision in a particle-free environment. Someone must machine the aluminum vacuum chambers, fire the ceramic electrostatic chucks, distribute the ultra-pure chemicals, supply the inspection optics, and fabricate the springs inside every probe card.

These companies rarely appear in semiconductor industry headlines. Their products are not photoresist or EUV light sources; they are the bolts, rails, robots, chucks, substrates, and logistics networks that connect everything else together. Without them, a $20 billion fab is an empty shell. With them, it becomes the most complex manufacturing environment ever constructed by human beings.

What makes this layer of the supply chain remarkable is its concentration. The vast majority of these enabling companies are Japanese, and many hold dominant or near-monopoly positions in their respective niches. Daifuku controls the cleanroom transport arteries. Rorze builds the wafer-handling robots that equipment makers integrate into their tools. Naigai Tech and Marumae machine the precision parts that Tokyo Electron cannot source elsewhere. NGK Insulators and Kyocera supply ceramic components without which plasma processing would be impossible. Niterra provides IC package substrates. Nagase distributes the chemicals that keep the entire front-end running. Aval Data and NHK Spring supply the subsystems that make semiconductor testing possible. And Olympus, better known for endoscopes, quietly provides the microscopy and metrology instruments that verify everything works.

This chapter maps the factory behind the factory.

## The Infrastructure Challenge: Moving, Holding, Feeding, Testing

A modern 300mm semiconductor fab processes wafers through an average of 700 to 1,000 discrete steps. At the 3nm node, a single wafer may spend 60 to 90 days inside the fab before it emerges as finished silicon. During that time, it must be transported between dozens of different tools, loaded and unloaded with micron-level accuracy, held rigidly in place during plasma bombardment or chemical exposure, and inspected at multiple checkpoints to verify that each layer is correctly formed.

The infrastructure challenge divides into five functional categories:

**Automation and handling.** Moving wafers between process tools through overhead transport systems and loading them into equipment via robotic arms. This is the circulatory system of the fab.

**Precision parts and components.** Manufacturing the vacuum chambers, electrostatic chucks, ceramic substrates, and machined metal parts that go inside every piece of process equipment. These are the bones and organs.

**Chemical distribution.** Sourcing, purifying, and delivering the photoresists, etchants, solvents, and specialty chemicals that each process step consumes. This is the bloodstream.

**Test subsystems.** Building the probe cards, tester boards, image processing modules, and mechanical components that enable wafer-level testing. This is the nervous system.

**Metrology and inspection.** Providing the microscopes and measurement instruments that verify dimensional accuracy, detect defects, and ensure process control. These are the eyes.

In each of these categories, Japanese companies hold positions that range from significant to dominant. We examine them in turn.

## Automation and Handling

### Daifuku Co., Ltd. (6383.T) -- The Circulatory System of Every Advanced Fab

**Founded:** 1937 | **HQ:** Osaka, Japan | **Market Cap:** ~1.85 trillion yen

Daifuku is the world's largest manufacturer of material handling systems, and its semiconductor division -- the cleanroom AMHS (Automated Material Handling System) business -- holds a near-monopoly in advanced-node fabs. Every wafer that moves between process steps in a leading-edge TSMC, Samsung, or Intel facility travels on a Daifuku overhead hoist transport (OHT) rail system.

The company's origins date to 1937, when it was founded as Daifuku Kiko to manufacture chain conveyors. Over the decades, it expanded into factory automation across multiple industries: automotive assembly lines, airport baggage handling, warehouse logistics, and eventually semiconductor cleanrooms. The semiconductor AMHS business, marketed under the Cleanway brand, became the company's highest-margin and fastest-growing segment as fab complexity escalated through the 2010s and 2020s.

The physics of semiconductor AMHS are exacting. Wafers travel inside Front Opening Unified Pods (FOUPs) that are suspended from overhead rails and move between process bays at controlled speeds. The OHT vehicles must operate in ISO Class 1 cleanroom conditions, generating zero particles, while maintaining positional accuracy sufficient to dock with tool load ports. In a modern fab, there may be several hundred OHT vehicles running simultaneously on kilometers of rail, all coordinated by Daifuku's proprietary traffic management software.

What makes Daifuku's position so defensible is the switching cost. An AMHS installation is integral to the fab's physical structure -- the rails are mounted to the ceiling, the routing is designed around the tool layout, and the software is deeply integrated with the fab's manufacturing execution system (MES). Replacing an AMHS vendor would require shutting down the entire fab. No chipmaker has ever done this. Once Daifuku wins the initial contract for a new fab, it captures the aftermarket revenue for the fab's entire 20-to-30-year operating life.

Daifuku supplies AMHS to TSMC, Samsung, and Intel -- the three companies that collectively account for over 80% of leading-edge semiconductor capital expenditure. The company also has subsidiaries in Korea (Clean Factomation) and China (DSA) to serve local customers. As the global fab construction boom accelerates -- driven by TSMC's Arizona expansion, Samsung's Taylor, Texas fab, Intel's Ohio mega-site, and Japan's own Rapidus project in Hokkaido -- Daifuku is positioned to capture AMHS orders from every one of these projects.

### Rorze Corporation (6323.T) -- The Hands Inside the Tool

**Founded:** 1985 | **HQ:** Fukuyama, Hiroshima | **Market Cap:** ~570 billion yen

If Daifuku builds the highways between process tools, Rorze builds the hands that load and unload wafers at each stop. The company is one of the world's leading manufacturers of wafer transfer robots -- the precision robotic arms that extract 300mm wafers from FOUPs, place them onto process chucks, and return them after processing, all without introducing a single particle of contamination.

Rorze was founded in 1985 in Fukuyama, a mid-sized city in Hiroshima Prefecture, an unlikely birthplace for a company that would become integral to the global semiconductor supply chain. The company built its reputation on dust-free transport robotics, developing vacuum-compatible and cleanroom-grade robotic systems that equipment makers like Tokyo Electron and SCREEN Holdings integrate into their own tools. Rorze also sells directly to fabs, including Samsung and TSMC.

The technical requirements for wafer transfer robots are extreme. The robot must position a 300mm wafer -- worth potentially tens of thousands of dollars in accumulated processing value -- with sub-50-micrometer accuracy, at speeds sufficient to maintain tool throughput, in a vacuum or ultra-clean atmospheric environment. The end effector (the "hand" that holds the wafer) must grip without touching the device-active surface, typically using edge contact or electrostatic methods. And the entire mechanism must generate zero particles -- no lubricant outgassing, no metal-on-metal friction, no vibration-induced flaking.

Rorze's client list reads like a who's who of semiconductor manufacturing. The company supplies wafer transfer robots to Tokyo Electron (CMP-0004) and SCREEN Holdings (CMP-0003) -- two of the largest semiconductor equipment manufacturers in the world -- as well as directly to TSMC and Samsung. Its supply chain graph edges confirm supplier relationships with all four of these companies.

### Marumae Co., Ltd. (6264.T) -- Precision Machining for Vacuum Chambers

**Founded:** 1961 | **HQ:** Kagoshima, Japan | **Market Cap:** ~25.4 billion yen

Marumae is a precision contract manufacturer specializing in aluminum vacuum chambers, electrodes, and other machined parts for semiconductor and flat-panel display manufacturing equipment. The company holds the distinction of being recognized as a Tokyo Electron "E-COMPASS Green Partner" -- a designation reserved for TEL's most trusted and environmentally compliant suppliers.

Vacuum chambers are among the most critical structural components in semiconductor process equipment. Every etch chamber, CVD reactor, and PVD sputtering system requires a precision-machined vacuum enclosure capable of maintaining pressures as low as 10^-7 Torr while withstanding the corrosive effects of fluorine-based and chlorine-based process gases. The chambers must be machined from high-purity aluminum alloys, with internal surface finishes polished to minimize particle generation and gas adsorption. Dimensional tolerances are typically in the tens of micrometers -- tight enough to ensure proper sealing of o-ring grooves and gas inlet geometries, but over chamber dimensions that can exceed one meter.

Marumae's strength lies in its capability for small-lot, multi-variety machining -- the ability to produce dozens of different chamber configurations, each in quantities of just a few units, with the quality and traceability that semiconductor equipment makers require. The company supplies vacuum chambers to both Tokyo Electron (CMP-0004) and SCREEN Holdings (CMP-0003), as confirmed by the supply chain graph edges.

From its base in Kagoshima Prefecture -- at the southern tip of Kyushu, far from Japan's traditional industrial heartland -- Marumae has built a fast-growing business. Revenue grew 40% year-over-year in the latest fiscal year, reaching approximately 8.0 billion yen, with an operating margin of 20.9%. This growth rate far exceeds the G1 peer median of 15.6% and reflects the surge in semiconductor equipment demand driven by the global fab construction cycle.

### NGK Insulators, Ltd. (5333.T) -- The Ceramic Backbone of Plasma Processing

**Founded:** 1919 | **HQ:** Nagoya, Japan | **Market Cap:** ~1.18 trillion yen

NGK Insulators is one of the world's most important ceramics companies, and its semiconductor business -- though a minority of total revenue -- supplies a component without which plasma-based processing would be impossible: the electrostatic chuck (ESC).

An electrostatic chuck is the device that holds a wafer in place during plasma processing. Unlike a mechanical clamp, which would touch the wafer's front surface and generate particles, an ESC uses an electric field to attract the wafer against a ceramic surface via Coulomb or Johnsen-Rahbek forces. The wafer is held flat and immobile during plasma bombardment, with helium gas flowing through micro-channels in the chuck to provide thermal coupling between the wafer and a temperature-controlled pedestal below. The ESC must be made from a material that is electrically insulating (to maintain the electrostatic field), thermally conductive (to remove heat from the wafer), chemically inert (to resist fluorine and chlorine plasma chemistries), and mechanically flat to sub-micrometer tolerances.

Aluminum nitride (AlN) ceramic is the material of choice, and NGK Insulators is a world leader in AlN technology. The company has been manufacturing ceramics since 1919, when it was spun off from the insulator division of NGK (Nippon Gaishi), originally a maker of electrical power transmission insulators. Over the century that followed, NGK developed proprietary sintering techniques for advanced ceramics that achieve the combination of purity, density, and thermal conductivity that semiconductor applications demand.

NGK's electrostatic chucks are supplied to both Tokyo Electron (CMP-0004) and SCREEN Holdings (CMP-0003), as well as directly to major foundries including TSMC and Samsung. The supply chain graph confirms edges to all four of these companies. Every etch step, every CVD deposition step, and many cleaning steps in the semiconductor process require an ESC -- and at leading-edge nodes with hundreds of etch and deposition cycles per wafer, a single fab may contain thousands of ESC units across its tool fleet.

Beyond electrostatic chucks, NGK supplies ceramic heaters -- used to maintain precise wafer temperatures during thermal processing -- and ceramic components for other equipment subsystems. The company's broader business includes NAS (sodium-sulfur) batteries for grid energy storage, automotive ceramic substrates for catalytic converters, and industrial process equipment, making its semiconductor exposure one segment within a diversified portfolio.

### Kyocera Corporation (6971.T) -- The Ceramics Conglomerate

**Founded:** 1959 | **HQ:** Kyoto, Japan | **Market Cap:** ~3.5 trillion yen

Kyocera is a diversified Japanese conglomerate whose semiconductor relevance stems from its original business: fine ceramics. Founded in 1959 by Kazuo Inamori -- one of Japan's most celebrated entrepreneurs, who also founded KDDI and later rescued Japan Airlines -- Kyocera began as a manufacturer of ceramic components for television picture tubes. By 1964, the company had pivoted to semiconductor ceramic packages, and it has supplied the semiconductor industry continuously for over sixty years.

Kyocera's semiconductor-related products span multiple categories. Ceramic packages house individual IC chips, providing electrical connections, thermal management, and physical protection. Organic packages serve similar functions using laminated resin substrates rather than ceramics. The company also produces crystal oscillators (timing devices essential for every electronic system), lead frames, and substrate materials. More recently, Kyocera's fine ceramic components are used inside semiconductor process equipment itself -- as parts for plasma chambers, wafer stages, and other subsystems that require the thermal stability, chemical resistance, and electrical properties that only advanced ceramics can provide.

The company is vast. With revenue of approximately 2 trillion yen across divisions including solar energy, telecommunications equipment, printers, cutting tools, and medical devices, Kyocera's semiconductor components business represents a fraction of its total portfolio. The Core Components segment (which includes semiconductor packages and electronic components) accounts for roughly 28% of revenue, with Electronic Components at 18% and Solutions (printers, telecom) at 54%.

This diversification is both a strength and a limitation for semiconductor-focused investors. Kyocera's ceramic technology base is world-class, and its semiconductor packages are found in applications ranging from automotive to aerospace. But the semiconductor business is diluted within a much larger conglomerate, making it difficult to isolate semiconductor-specific growth or valuation metrics.

## Chemical Distribution

### Nagase & Co., Ltd. (8012.T) -- The Supply Chain's Logistics Nerve Center

**Founded:** 1832 | **HQ:** Osaka, Japan | **Market Cap:** ~456 billion yen

Nagase is a Japanese trading company -- a sogo shosha in miniature -- that has evolved from a 190-year-old dye merchant into one of the semiconductor industry's most important chemical distributors. The company serves as a critical intermediary between specialty chemical manufacturers and the fabs that consume their products, handling procurement, purification, quality assurance, logistics, and technical support.

Founded in 1832 during the late Edo period as a dealer in dyestuffs and chemicals, Nagase has been a chemicals trading company for nearly two centuries. Its semiconductor relevance centers on the electronic materials distribution business, where it acts as the distribution partner for major photoresist and specialty chemical producers. The supply chain graph confirms supplier relationships with Tokyo Ohka Kogyo (CMP-0033) and JSR (CMP-0034) -- Japan's two largest photoresist manufacturers -- as well as a direct supply edge to TSMC for electronic chemicals.

But Nagase is more than a logistics company. Its subsidiary Nagase ChemteX manufactures modified epoxy resins used in advanced semiconductor packaging -- the adhesives and encapsulants that bond dies to substrates and protect them from the environment. As AI server chip packaging has grown more complex (with multi-chip modules, high-bandwidth memory stacks, and silicon interposers), the demand for high-performance epoxy formulations has surged, and Nagase ChemteX is positioned as a key supplier.

The chemical distribution role that Nagase plays is often underestimated. Semiconductor-grade chemicals must maintain purity levels measured in parts per billion or parts per trillion during transport and storage. Nagase manages the specialized containers, clean delivery infrastructure, and quality verification protocols that ensure chemicals arrive at the fab in specification. For a chemical producer like TOK or JSR, outsourcing distribution to Nagase allows them to focus on formulation and synthesis while relying on a partner with deep logistics expertise and established relationships at every major fab.

### NHK Spring Co., Ltd. (5991.T) -- From Automotive Springs to Semiconductor Precision Parts

**Founded:** 1939 | **HQ:** Yokohama, Japan | **Market Cap:** ~634.5 billion yen

NHK Spring -- marketed under the Nippatus brand -- is Japan's largest spring manufacturer and a world leader in hard disk drive (HDD) suspensions, commanding approximately 50% global market share in the micro-precision suspensions that position read/write heads above spinning disk platters. But it is the company's expanding semiconductor process parts business that earns it a place in this chapter.

The connection between springs and semiconductors is less improbable than it might appear. The core competency that NHK Spring has developed over eight decades -- the ability to manufacture precision metal components at microscopic scales with extreme consistency -- translates directly to semiconductor applications. Probe cards, the devices used to electrically test individual die on a wafer, require thousands of tiny spring-loaded pins (cantilevers or vertical probes) that must make contact with bond pads measuring tens of micrometers across. NHK Spring supplies the precision spring components that go into probe cards built by companies like FormFactor and its Japanese competitors.

The supply chain graph confirms that NHK Spring supplies probe card springs to Advantest (CMP-0005), one of the world's two dominant semiconductor test equipment manufacturers. Additionally, NHK Spring supplies HDD suspensions to TSMC (CMP-0017) -- likely through TSMC's data storage and server infrastructure -- where it commands 50% market share.

Beyond probe card components, NHK Spring's semiconductor process parts business -- which uses the company's proprietary metal bonding technology -- is growing rapidly. The company manufactures etching and deposition parts (chamber components, gas delivery parts, electrode assemblies) for semiconductor equipment makers. This business leverages the same metallurgical expertise developed for automotive and HDD applications but applies it to the tolerances and purity requirements of semiconductor manufacturing.

## Metrology and Inspection

### Olympus Corporation (7733.T) -- Precision Measurement for Process Control

**Founded:** 1919 | **HQ:** Hachioji, Tokyo | **Market Cap:** ~2.12 trillion yen

Olympus is known globally for two things: medical endoscopes, where it holds dominant market share, and cameras, a consumer business it exited in 2021. But the company's Scientific Solutions segment -- which includes industrial microscopes, measuring instruments, and non-destructive testing (NDT) equipment -- plays a supporting role in semiconductor manufacturing that merits inclusion in this chapter.

Semiconductor fabs use optical microscopes extensively for process development, failure analysis, and quality control. When a process engineer needs to visually inspect a wafer surface, measure the width of a patterned feature, examine the cross-section of a via or contact, or analyze a defect captured by an in-line inspection tool, the instrument of choice is typically a high-end optical or confocal microscope. Olympus (and its primary competitor, the German firm Zeiss) supplies these instruments to fab clean rooms worldwide.

Olympus's industrial microscopes incorporate technologies developed across the company's century-long history in optics: specialized objective lenses designed for semiconductor feature sizes, confocal scanning systems that can measure three-dimensional surface topography, and digital imaging platforms that integrate with fab data management systems. The company's NDT (non-destructive testing) equipment -- including ultrasonic and X-ray inspection systems -- can be applied to semiconductor packaging inspection and failure analysis.

That said, Olympus's semiconductor exposure is modest relative to its total business. The company generates approximately 997.3 billion yen in annual revenue, the vast majority of which comes from its medical devices segments (Endoscopic Solutions and Therapeutic Solutions). The Scientific Solutions segment that serves semiconductor customers is a small fraction of the whole. Olympus is included in this chapter not because it is a major semiconductor revenue story, but because its instruments are physically present in virtually every fab and packaging facility in the world -- part of the invisible infrastructure that enables process engineers to see what they are building.

## Competitive Landscape

The companies profiled in this chapter face competition that varies dramatically by segment.

**Automation and handling.** Daifuku's AMHS dominance is challenged primarily by Murata Machinery (Muratec) in Japan and, to a lesser extent, by Brooks Automation (now Azenta) in the United States. However, at advanced nodes below 7nm, Daifuku's market share approaches monopoly levels. Rorze competes with Yaskawa Electric and Brooks Automation in wafer transfer robotics, but its cleanroom specialization and deep integration with major equipment makers provide a defensible position.

**Precision parts.** Naigai Tech and Marumae operate in a fragmented market of small-to-mid-sized Japanese precision machining firms. Their competitive advantage is not unique technology but rather decades of qualification and integration with specific equipment makers (primarily TEL). Switching a precision parts supplier requires requalification of potentially hundreds of individual part numbers -- a process that can take 12 to 18 months and carries yield risk.

**Ceramics.** NGK Insulators, Kyocera, and Niterra (NTK Ceratec) compete with each other and with international players including CoorsTek (USA) and Shinko Electric Industries (Japan) in various ceramic component categories. In electrostatic chucks specifically, NGK Insulators and Shinko are the primary suppliers, with competition limited by the extreme difficulty of achieving the required purity, flatness, and thermal properties in aluminum nitride ceramics.

**Chemical distribution.** Nagase competes with other Japanese trading companies (Itochu, Mitsubishi Chemical, Mitsui) in chemical distribution, but its specialized focus on electronic materials and its subsidiary Nagase ChemteX's manufacturing capability create differentiation beyond pure logistics.

**Test subsystems.** Aval Data competes with various industrial electronics firms but operates in niche segments (near-infrared imaging, custom board manufacturing) where its customer relationships provide protection. NHK Spring's probe card springs compete with products from Leeno Industrial (South Korea, CMP-0082) and FormFactor (USA), but the precision mechanical requirements create meaningful qualification barriers.

**Metrology.** Olympus competes with Carl Zeiss (Germany) in optical microscopy, and both companies compete with KLA Corporation (USA) and Hitachi High-Tech (Japan) in semiconductor inspection and metrology. Olympus's position in this space is primarily in offline measurement and failure analysis rather than in-line high-volume inspection, where KLA dominates.

## Supply Chain Connections

The companies in this chapter occupy a distinctive position in the semiconductor supply chain: most of them supply other Japanese equipment makers rather than fabs directly. This creates a layered structure in which infrastructure companies feed into equipment companies, which in turn feed into foundries and IDMs.

**Upstream dependencies.** Naigai Tech, Marumae, NGK Insulators, Niterra, and Aval Data all supply Tokyo Electron (CMP-0004) -- the world's third-largest semiconductor equipment maker and the dominant Japanese producer. This TEL-centric supply structure means that the fortunes of these infrastructure companies are tied not only to the global semiconductor cycle but specifically to TEL's order intake and production schedules. Marumae and Rorze also supply SCREEN Holdings (CMP-0003), Japan's leading wet cleaning equipment maker.

**Downstream reach.** Daifuku, Rorze, Nagase, NGK Insulators, Niterra, and NHK Spring supply directly to the major foundries and IDMs -- TSMC (CMP-0017), Samsung (CMP-0018), and Intel (CMP-0019). These direct relationships are confirmed by the supply chain graph edges. Daifuku's AMHS installations at all three companies represent some of the most strategically important supplier relationships in the entire semiconductor supply chain.

**Cross-chapter connections.** Several companies in this chapter also appear in adjacent chapters. Nagase's chemical distribution role connects to Chapter 8 (Photoresist and Chemicals), where it serves as the logistical bridge between photoresist formulators TOK and JSR and the fabs that consume their products. NHK Spring and Aval Data reappear in Chapter 13 (Wafer Test and Probing) as key subsystem suppliers to Advantest (CMP-0005). Niterra appears again in Chapter 15 (Advanced Packaging) as an IC package substrate supplier. These cross-chapter appearances underscore the interconnected nature of the semiconductor supply chain -- many infrastructure companies serve multiple process steps simultaneously.

The common thread connecting all eleven companies in this chapter is their invisibility to end consumers combined with their indispensability to the manufacturing process. No one buying a smartphone or GPU thinks about the overhead transport rails that moved the wafer between process tools, the aluminum vacuum chamber in which the transistor gate was etched, the ceramic chuck that held the wafer flat during plasma exposure, or the spring-loaded probe pin that verified the circuit worked. Yet without each of these components, the chip could not be made.

## Key Takeaways

- **A $15-25 billion fab requires all of these companies.** The headline equipment -- lithography scanners, etch chambers, deposition tools -- gets the attention, but the infrastructure layer (transport, handling, precision parts, chemicals logistics, test subsystems, metrology) is equally essential and often more difficult to substitute.

- **Daifuku's AMHS near-monopoly is the strongest competitive position in this chapter.** With perfect scores on market share and switching costs, and confirmed supply relationships with TSMC, Samsung, and Intel, Daifuku is structurally leveraged to every dollar of global fab capex. Its evaluation score of 58.5 reflects this dominance.

- **Customer concentration is the defining risk.** Naigai Tech (80% of sales to TEL), Marumae (primary customer TEL/SCREEN), and Aval Data (contract manufacturing for TEL/Nikon) all face binary risk from their dependence on a single or small number of customers. This concentration is the price of deep integration -- these companies know their customers' specifications intimately, but they have limited ability to diversify.

- **The ceramics triad -- NGK, Kyocera, Niterra -- collectively controls the ceramic components that semiconductor equipment cannot function without.** Electrostatic chucks, ceramic packages, heaters, and process chamber parts are all areas where Japanese ceramic technology is dominant, built on decades of materials science expertise that is difficult to replicate.

- **Valuation dispersion is extreme.** Within this single chapter, PER multiples range from 6.13x (Aval Data) to 32.22x (Marumae), and evaluation scores range from 43.9 (Marumae) to 59.5 (Olympus). The cheapest names on valuation metrics (Aval Data, Naigai Tech, NGK Insulators) tend to be the ones with the highest customer concentration risk or the most limited growth visibility -- a reminder that low multiples alone do not constitute an investment thesis.
